About this tool — what it tests, why, and what to look for

The question

Why do some videos stall at high playback speeds (8× / 16×) while others play smoothly, even when the bytes are fully cached and there's no network involved? Buffering at high speed is often blamed on slow networks, regions, or CDNs — this bench is here to rule those out and pin down where the bottleneck actually lives.

The hypothesis

It's not the network. It's per-machine video decoder throughput. At 16× playback on a 30 fps source, the decoder needs to deliver 480 frames per second; on a 50 fps source, 800 fps. Most consumer GPUs top out around 150–300 fps for H.264 1080p, so high-fps content stalls on weaker machines no matter how fast the bytes arrive.

This bench proves it by removing every network factor: videos load into IndexedDB, play from local memory via a plain <video> element, and the same buffering reproduces.

What WAITING means

HTML video elements fire a waiting event whenever playback advances faster than the decoder can deliver decoded frames. Two things to internalize:

  • It's not about bytes. The buffered stat may say "52.3s buffered" (whole file in RAM) and WAITING still fires repeatedly — because the bottleneck is decode, not download.
  • It scales with playback rate. A video that plays cleanly at 1× can fire dozens of WAITING events per second at 16×. Decode work scales linearly with rate.

So WAITING ≫ 0 at 16× = decoder bottleneck on this machine. WAITING ≈ 0 at 16× = decoder is keeping up.

How to use it

  1. Drag one or more videos into the drop zone.
  2. Open one. The Container & codec metadata card shows codec, fps, GOP size, and decode load at 1× / 16×. Look for ⚠ flags.
  3. Hit a speed (1× / 4× / 16×) and click ▶ Record test for a 10-second run. WAITING events get saved per video.
  4. For conclusive proof, click ⚙ Re-encode @ 15/30/60/120 fps on the active video. It runs ffmpeg.wasm in your browser to produce four variants of the same source, then opens a side-by-side comparison.
  5. In compare mode: pick 16×, hit ▶ Play all, then ▶ Record 10s test (all). Watch WAITING counts diverge — same machine, same zero-network playback, only fps differs.

What "good" and "bad" look like

  • Smooth at 16×: ≤15 fps, modest resolution, H.264. Decode load < ~400 MP/s at 16×.
  • Stalls at 16×: very wide / high-resolution sources, 30+ fps, very long GOP (>120 frames), or H.265/AV1 without hardware decode. Decode load > ~1500 MP/s at 16× usually exceeds consumer hardware.

The library badges (e.g. 1 test · max 16× · 73 WAITING) summarize each video's record. Compare badges across videos to see the pattern.

What this proves and what it doesn't

Proves: high-speed buffering is decoder-bound, not network-bound. Re-encoding to a lower fps fixes it; moving content closer to the user won't.

Doesn't address: first-load latency (cold start). That's a real network concern, and serving content from a closer region helps that separately. Two unrelated problems that look similar from the outside.

Caveats

  • WAITING counts depend on your machine's GPU/CPU. The same video tested on a faster laptop will show fewer WAITING events. That's the point.
  • Re-encoding runs in your browser via ffmpeg.wasm. It's slow (2–5 minutes for four variants of a 50-second clip). Coffee break time.
  • This page needs cross-origin isolation (COOP/COEP) for ffmpeg.wasm. The bundled service worker handles that automatically — first visit reloads once after registering.

How to read this: WAITING fires when the player advances faster than the decoder can deliver frames. With everything in memory, WAITING == decoder/CPU bottleneck. Compare videos with low vs high WAITING counts at the same speed; look at codec, resolution × fps × rate, avg GOP, and bitrate.
